Prawo Pracy

Polish employment law regulating employment contracts, wages, working hours, termination, and worker protections.

Polish employment law (Prawo Pracy) is primarily codified in the Labor Code (Kodeks Pracy, KP) of 1974 and numerous amendments. It comprehensively regulates the relationship between employers and employees, establishing minimum standards for contracts, compensation, working time, leave entitlements, health and safety, and termination procedures. For employers operating in Poland, compliance with Prawo Pracy is non-negotiable; violations can result in substantial fines, back-wage claims, and civil liability.

Employment contracts in Poland must specify the position, place of work, compensation, working hours, and term (fixed or indefinite). Most employees work on indefinite contracts (umowy na czas nieokreślony), which can be terminated only for cause or through legal dismissal (wypowiedzenie) procedures. Fixed-term contracts (umowy na czas określony) may be used for temporary or project-based work but cannot exceed a maximum aggregate duration (typically 3 years, with limited extensions).

Minimum wage in Poland is set by the government and is adjusted annually. As of 2026, minimum wage is approximately 4,300 PLN monthly (subject to annual updates). Employers must pay wages on dates specified in the employment contract (typically monthly or bi-weekly). Deductions from wages are permitted only for taxes, social contributions, and specified penalties or debts. Illegal deductions or non-payment trigger claims for back wages plus statutory interest.

Standard working hours in Poland are 40 hours per week, though flexible or reduced hours can be contractually arranged. Employees are entitled to paid annual leave (urlop wypoczynkowy) — a minimum of 20 working days for most employees, increased to 26 days for employees with dangerous or particularly onerous jobs. Public holidays are recognized; work on public holidays requires additional compensation or compensatory time off.

Termination of an indefinite employment contract requires either just cause (for dismissal — zwolnienie dyscyplinarne) or notice (wypowiedzenie) with a statutory notice period (typically 2 weeks from the employee; 2 weeks or longer from the employer depending on the contract). Wrongful termination claims can result in reinstatement, back pay, or damages. During probationary periods (up to 3 months), either party may terminate with one week's notice.

Key Facts

  • Minimum wage: approx. 4,300 PLN (updated annually)
  • Standard working week: 40 hours
  • Annual paid leave: minimum 20 working days
  • Notice periods: 2 weeks from employee; 2+ weeks from employer
  • Fixed-term contracts: max. 3 years aggregate duration

Common Mistake

Employers often attempt to circumvent Prawo Pracy protections through bogus independent contractor arrangements or repeated fixed-term contracts. Polish labor courts consistently recharacterize such arrangements as employment relationships, exposing employers to back-wage claims and penalties.

Extra Tip

When terminating an employee, follow statutory notice procedures precisely. Improper notice or dismissal can result in wrongful termination claims. Consult a Polish employment lawyer before terminating any employee to ensure compliance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we use independent contractor arrangements to avoid Prawo Pracy?

Not reliably. Polish courts examine the substance of the relationship. If the "contractor" works exclusively for your company, on your schedule, using your equipment, they will likely be recharacterized as an employee.

What probationary rights do employees have?

During probation (do 3 months), either party may terminate with one week's notice. After probation, the standard notice periods (2+ weeks) apply.

Are work-from-home arrangements permitted?

Yes. Remote work can be specified in the contract. Employers remain responsible for health and safety compliance and must provide necessary equipment and support.
