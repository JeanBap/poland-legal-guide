Prawo Rodzinne

Polish family law governing marriage, divorce, child custody, inheritance, and parental rights.

Polish family law (Prawo Rodzinne) is codified primarily in the Family and Guardianship Code (Kodeks Rodzinny i Opiekuńczy, KRiO) of 1964 and subsequent amendments. It comprehensively regulates marriage formation and dissolution, parental rights, guardianship, inheritance upon death of family members, and spousal property regimes. For expat families in Poland, understanding these rules is essential because they govern property division upon divorce, child custody allocation, and inheritance distribution regardless of nationality.

Marriage in Poland is a civil contract, solemnized before a registrar (urząd stanu cywilnego) or, under church law provisions, in an authorized religious ceremony with subsequent civil registration. Parties to a marriage have default property rights under the regime of joint marital property (majątek wspólny) — acquired property is presumed jointly owned unless separated by specific marital property agreement (umowa majątkowa). For international couples, Polish law permits choice of law agreements specifying which country's law governs property rights, but default Polish law applies unless an agreement is registered.

Divorce in Poland is granted only if the marriage has broken down irretrievably. Neither "no-fault" nor "at-fault" provisions exist in modern Polish law; instead, courts assess whether there is a "complete and permanent breakdown of marital ties" (zupełny i trwały rozkład pożycia małżeńskiego). Grounds for divorce include living separately, infidelity, abuse, alcoholism, or other conduct destroying the marriage foundation. The process can be relatively swift if both parties agree (contested divorces take 1–3 years).

Upon divorce, joint marital property is divided "fairly" — not necessarily 50/50, but equitably considering each party's contributions, incapacity, and best interests of children. Child custody is awarded based on the child's best interests (interes dziecka), with courts preferring joint custody (władza rodzicielska) but awarding sole or alternating custody where appropriate. Child support obligations continue until the child reaches age 18 (or 25 if in full-time education).

Inheritance rights are determined by Polish law based on blood relationship and the existence of a valid will. If no will exists, the intestate succession rules of the Kodeks Rodzinny apply: children and spouses have priority claims, followed by parents, then more distant relatives. Foreign nationals in Poland can inherit and are treated identically to Polish nationals.

Key Facts

  • Governed by Kodeks Rodzinny i Opiekuńczy (Family and Guardianship Code)
  • Default property regime: joint marital property (can be modified by agreement)
  • Marriage dissolution requires proof of "complete and permanent breakdown"
  • Divorce settlements address property division and child custody/support
  • Intestate succession follows statutory hierarchy; wills can vary distribution

Common Mistake

Expats marrying in Poland often overlook the implications of joint marital property. Without a prenuptial agreement (umowa majątkowa), future acquisitions are automatically jointly owned, even if titled in one spouse's name. Execute a property agreement before marriage if you wish to maintain separate property.

Extra Tip

If you marry in Poland and intend to divorce, or if you have children, register a choice of law agreement specifying which country's law will govern your family rights. Polish courts will respect such agreements, and they provide predictability for international couples.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we use a prenuptial agreement in Poland?

Yes. A marital property agreement (umowa majątkowa) can be executed before or after marriage. It must be notarized and registered to be enforceable.

How is custody determined for international couples?

Polish courts apply the "best interests of the child" standard and may consider the child's connections to Poland, the stability of each household, and the child's wishes. Courts typically award joint custody unless one parent is deemed unfit.

Can a foreign will be valid in Poland?

Foreign wills are recognized if they comply with the law of the testator's domicile or nationality. However, registering a Polish will is simpler; a Polish lawyer can prepare one after you die.
