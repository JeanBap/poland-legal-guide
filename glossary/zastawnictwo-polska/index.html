Zastawnictwo

Polish pledging and collateral law governing security interests in movable and immovable property.

Zastawnictwo (the law of pledge and collateral) governs the creation, registration, and enforcement of security interests in Polish property. It is essential to understand because it determines creditor rights when borrowers default and affects the marketability and value of property. Both movable property (chattels) and immovable property (real estate) can be pledged as security, though the registration and enforcement mechanisms differ significantly.

For movable collateral (ruchomości) — vehicles, equipment, inventory — a pledge is created by a written agreement specifying the debtor, creditor, secured claim amount, and the collateral. The pledge does not require transfer of possession (a key distinction from older common law security). However, registration of the pledge is increasingly required; movable pledges are registered in the National Register of Debts (Krajowy Rejestr Długów) or, for certain assets like vehicles, in specialized registers (Central Motor Vehicle Register). An unregistered pledge may be enforceable between the parties but is subordinate to registered pledges and third-party rights.

For immovable collateral (nieruchomości) — land and buildings — a mortgage (hipoteka) is the standard security interest. A mortgage is created by notarial agreement and must be registered in the Land and Mortgage Register (Księga Wieczysta) to be effective. The mortgage secures a specific debt amount and binds successors in title — if the property is sold, the mortgage passes to the new owner unless discharged.

Enforcement of a pledge on movable property follows the terms of the pledge agreement or, in default, seizure and public sale. For mortgages on immovable property, the creditor has the right to commence foreclosure proceedings (egzekucja) before a court, typically resulting in forced sale of the property with proceeds applied to satisfy the debt.

Subordination of pledges and mortgages follows registration priority. The first-registered security interest has priority; subsequent creditors have subordinate claims. This registration priority system incentivizes formal registration and provides transparency to all parties.

Key Facts

  • Pledge agreement required (written); registration strengthens enforceability
  • Mortgages on real estate must be registered in Land Register (Księga Wieczysta)
  • Priority follows registration order — first-registered has priority
  • Enforcement: public sale for mortgages; seizure/sale for movable pledges
  • Pledges do not require transfer of possession

Common Mistake

Lenders accepting oral pledges on movable property or failing to register mortgages on real estate. Unregistered or informal pledges are subordinate to formal registered interests and may be unenforceable if the debtor becomes insolvent.

Extra Tip

When obtaining financing in Poland, ensure that any security agreement is notarized and registered immediately. An unregistered pledge is at risk of being subordinated by subsequent creditors.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a pledge be created on a vehicle already mortgaged?

Yes, but the pledge will be subordinate to the mortgage. Creditors have claims in order of registration priority.

How long does it take to foreclose a mortgage in Poland?

Court foreclosure typically takes 12–24 months. Auction proceedings and creditor claims can extend timelines further.

What happens to pledges if the debtor declares bankruptcy?

Pledged property may be seized and sold to satisfy the secured debt. Unsecured creditors receive only residual proceeds, if any.
